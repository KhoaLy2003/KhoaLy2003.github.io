A desk doesn't need to be big to feel finished — it just needs every piece to earn its spot. For months, my corner setup was functional but unfinished: a laptop perched on a stand, a cramped keyboard, and an awkward gap where a second screen should have been. That gap is closed now, and the whole desk finally feels like a workspace instead of a temporary arrangement.

## Why the Screen Was the Missing Piece

Working off a single laptop display in a small room sounds manageable until you actually try to do it for a full day. Every window overlap, every alt-tab between a browser and a document, chips away at focus. The desk itself was never the problem — it was the lack of a second plane to put things on.

That's why the centerpiece of this update is the **Xiaomi Monitor A24i 2026**, a 24-inch display that now sits front and center on an adjustable stand. It's not a flashy ultrawide or a curved gaming panel — it's simply a clean, sharp screen that gives the laptop somewhere to send its overflow. The laptop itself now sits slightly behind and to the side, raised on a stand, acting almost like a secondary display rather than the main one. That single change rearranged the whole flow of the desk.

## A Keyboard That Matches the Space

Small desks punish big keyboards. A full-size board with a number pad doesn't just take up width — it pushes the mouse out wide, and pushes *you* further from the monitor than you'd like to sit. The **Leaven K68** solves this by being a 65%-style compact mechanical keyboard: no number pad, minimal bezels, but still a satisfying tactile feel for typing.

The real win isn't just the desk space it frees up — it's what that space gets used for instead. With the keyboard shrunk down, there's now room for a small phone stand, a notebook, and a portable charger to sit comfortably to the left, all within easy reach without crowding the monitor's base.

<Callout type="tip">
If you're working with a desk under 100cm wide, a compact (65% or 60%) keyboard is one of the highest-impact upgrades you can make — it reclaims more usable surface area than almost anything else.
</Callout>

That extra breathing room on the desk needed a mouse to match — something that wouldn't reintroduce the bulk the keyboard had just eliminated.

## Quiet, Compact, and Out of the Way

The **Ugreen MU006** Bluetooth mouse rounds out the input setup. It's a silent-click mouse, which matters more than it sounds like it would in a small room — especially one where the desk shares space with a curtain-drawn sleeping area and not much sound insulation. No cable to manage, no clicking that carries through the room late at night, and a shape that sits naturally next to the compact keyboard without looking oversized.

It's a small, almost invisible upgrade on its own. But paired with the keyboard, it means the entire input area now takes up roughly the same footprint as a single full-size keyboard used to — freeing up the rest of the desk for the monitor stand, a water bottle, and whatever the day's work happens to need.

## The Bigger Picture

Step back from the desk and the second photo tells the real story — a narrow corner, a single window's worth of wall space, and a setup that had to fit *around* a bookshelf, a curtain, and a stack of storage boxes rather than the other way around. That's the real constraint most desk setups in small rooms face: not "what's the dream configuration," but "what fits."

<Figure src="public/images/blog/1838.jpg" alt="Wide view of the desk setup tucked into a corner of the room" caption="The full corner: desk, monitor, and chair fitted into the available space" />

Adding the Xiaomi A24i didn't mean buying a bigger desk or rearranging the room — it meant choosing peripherals that made the existing footprint work harder. The compact keyboard and mouse weren't just stylistic choices; they were what made room for the monitor in the first place.

## What's Next

The setup is functional now, but "finished" is doing some heavy lifting in that title — cable management around the power strip is still very much a work in progress, and a proper monitor arm could free up even more desk depth down the line. For now though, the gap is closed, the second screen is doing real work, and the desk finally feels like it was *designed*, not just assembled.
